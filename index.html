DISCUSSION

Wrapping Up

Women played a crucial role in the development of software engineering through groups such as the ENIAC teams that we discussed in this course. We can also see this in popular culture through media such as the movie Hidden Figures about the black women “computers” in NASA’s early days and through the novel Code Girls about the women working to crack ciphers for the US military during World War 2. These two pieces of media in particular have always stuck with me because they brought to light an essential part of the computing story that I had never been told. It’s fascinating to learn about how much women contributed to the early days of computing; the “When Computers were Women” reading even stated that in the ENIAC project “designing hardware was a man’s job; programming was a woman’s job”. Today, we find that a huge majority of software engineering jobs are filled by men. Women are participating in the field, but at much smaller percentages than men.

As a woman in computing, I am empowered by these stories of women contributing to the early days of computing. Women’s relationship to code should be one of inclusion. We should continue to share the full history of computing, highlighting the contributions that weren’t previously noted, and bring everyone into the technical world by making resources for coding accessible to everyone. Having a more diverse group of technologists will lead to more creative projects and advancements. This course invites us to consider how all of humanity interacts with technology and one way we can do that is to acknowledge the complex relationship women have with the computing field.
